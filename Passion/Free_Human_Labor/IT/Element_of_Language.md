If we were to write an **"Elements of the English Language"** in the style of Euclid, we would not start with grammar rules. We would start with the most fundamental objects of language, then establish axioms (postulates), then common notions from which grammar, rhetoric, literature, and communication could be derived.

# Elements of the English Language

## Book I — Foundations of Language

# Definitions

### Definition 1 — Sound

A sound is a perceptible vibration used in speech.

---

### Definition 2 — Symbol

A symbol is a mark used to represent information.

---

### Definition 3 — Letter

A letter is a symbol representing one or more sounds in a language.

Examples:

```text
A B C D ... Z
```

---

### Definition 4 — Word

A word is a meaningful unit of language composed of one or more symbols.

Examples:

```text
tree
run
beautiful
```

---

### Definition 5 — Meaning

Meaning is the concept or idea associated with a word, phrase, or expression.

---

### Definition 6 — Sentence

A sentence is an ordered collection of words expressing a complete thought.

---

### Definition 7 — Speaker

A speaker is an agent that produces language.

---

### Definition 8 — Listener

A listener is an agent that interprets language.

---

### Definition 9 — Communication

Communication is the transmission of meaning between agents through language.

---

### Definition 10 — Grammar

Grammar is the system of rules governing the structure of language.

---

### Definition 11 — Phrase

A phrase is a group of words functioning as a unit within a sentence.

---

### Definition 12 — Clause

A clause is a group of words containing a subject and a predicate.

---

### Definition 13 — Subject

The subject is that about which something is stated.

---

### Definition 14 — Predicate

The predicate is that which is stated about the subject.

---

### Definition 15 — Context

Context is the surrounding information that influences interpretation.

---

### Definition 16 — Language

Language is a structured system of symbols used to represent and communicate meaning.

---

# Postulates of Language

These are accepted without proof.

## Postulate 1

Every act of language intends to convey meaning.

---

## Postulate 2

Meaning may be represented by symbols.

---

## Postulate 3

Symbols may be combined into larger structures.

Examples:

```text
Letter → Word
Word → Phrase
Phrase → Sentence
Sentence → Discourse
```

---

## Postulate 4

The order of symbols may affect meaning.

Example:

```text
Dog bites man.
Man bites dog.
```

---

## Postulate 5

A statement may be understood only if speaker and listener share sufficient conventions.

---

## Postulate 6

The same meaning may be expressed in multiple forms.

Example:

```text
I am hungry.
I'm hungry.
```

---

## Postulate 7

The same form may possess multiple meanings.

Example:

```text
bank
```

may mean:

* financial institution
* river bank

---

## Postulate 8

Language is finite in symbols but potentially infinite in expression.

From a small alphabet, infinitely many sentences may be formed.

---

## Postulate 9

Every utterance occurs within a context.

---

## Postulate 10

Language evolves through usage.

---

# Common Notions

These are universal principles of reasoning about language.

## Common Notion 1

Things that signify the same meaning are equivalent in meaning.

Example:

```text
car
automobile
```

---

## Common Notion 2

If two speakers use the same symbols according to the same rules, they may communicate.

---

## Common Notion 3

Removing information generally reduces certainty.

Example:

```text
The red car stopped.

↓

The car stopped.
```

Less information remains.

---

## Common Notion 4

Adding information generally increases specificity.

Example:

```text
dog

↓

large black dog
```

---

## Common Notion 5

The whole meaning of a sentence depends upon both its parts and their arrangement.

---

## Common Notion 6

A word derives meaning partly from its context.

Example:

```text
light luggage
light bulb
```

Different meanings of "light."

---

## Common Notion 7

Ambiguity exists whenever more than one interpretation is possible.

---

## Common Notion 8

Contradictory statements cannot both be true in the same sense at the same time.

Example:

```text
The door is open.
The door is not open.
```

---

## Common Notion 9

A definition establishes the intended meaning of a term.

---

## Common Notion 10

Language may describe itself.

Examples:

```text
"Noun" is a noun.

"The word 'run' has three letters."
```

This property is called self-reference.

---

# The Five Great Laws of Language

### Law 1

All language is symbolic.

### Law 2

All communication seeks the transfer of meaning.

### Law 3

Meaning depends upon both structure and context.

### Law 4

Finite rules generate infinite expressions.

### Law 5

Understanding requires shared conventions.

---

# Example Proposition I

**Proposition:** If two persons share a language and use its symbols according to common conventions, communication is possible.

**Proof:**

1. By Postulate 2, meaning may be represented by symbols.
2. By Postulate 5, shared conventions permit interpretation.
3. By Definition 9, communication is the transmission of meaning.
4. Therefore communication is possible. ∎

This mirrors Euclid's style: starting with a few primitive concepts and building an entire theory of English language, grammar, semantics, rhetoric, literature, and communication from them.
